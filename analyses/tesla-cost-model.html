The exact math — verify it yourself

Every number on this page comes from these formulas. Definitions: P = amount financed (price + tax + rolled $9k Kia gap − down) · i = APR ÷ 1200 (monthly rate) · n = loan months · m = the month in question · r = inflation / discount rate (annual) · df(t) = 1 ÷ (1 + r)t/12 = the present-value factor for a dollar spent at month t (df = 1 when r = 0).

1 · Monthly payment (standard amortization; if APR = 0, pmt = P/n) pmt  =  P · i1 − (1 + i)−n
2 · Loan balance still owed at month m  (0 once m ≥ n) b(m)  =  P(1 + i)m − pmt · (1 + i)m − 1i
3 · Monthly running cost  (gas, EV energy, or both for the PHEV) run = insurance + maintenance + energy + gas,    where   gas = miles/yr ÷ mpg × $/gal12   and EV energy = the charging-case value ($50 / $120 / $184)
4 · Total cash out by month m — each future month re-priced to today's dollars at rate r  (lease pays every month held) cash(m)  =  down + Σt=1..m ( pmt·[t ≤ n] + run ) · df(t)   (at r = 0 this collapses to down + pmt·min(m,n) + run·m)
5 · Equity (what the car is worth to you) — resale is straight-line between anchor months 0,12,24,36,48,60,72,96 equity(m) = resale(m) − b(m)   (= 0 for a lease — you own nothing)
6 · True cost in today's dollars (the "net of resale" line)  ·  and the two milestones net(m) = cash(m) − equity(m) · df(m)   |   Beats Kia = smallest m with networld < netKia   |   Walk-away-whole = smallest m with equity ≥ 0

Corrections already applied + the limits to keep in mind:

  • Fixed · gas default. Was $5.61 (a mid-2026 Hormuz spike); now $4.60 (WA multi-year baseline). The Kia is the most gas-sensitive world, so this is the single biggest lever — slider it to the price you expect to average.
  • Fixed · WA trade-in tax credit (toggle, on). WA taxes (price − trade-in allowance), so trading the ~$21k Kia saves ~$2,320 tax on every switch. Applied uniformly; favors switching, partly offsetting the gas fix.
  • Fixed · chart. Y-axis is locked to all worlds (hiding a line no longer re-slopes the rest); the right-edge hover dead-zone and end-label overlap are gone.
  • Rejected (false alarm). A validator claimed 4 EV rows carry no sales tax — they do: WA tax is baked into every switch's OTD price (e.g. MY 0.99% $64,011 = $51,880×1.1105 − $2,569 + $9,000). Not double-counted.
  • 6% used-MY APR is a best case (credit-union / promo). A conventional 720 buyer often sees ~9–10% — that's the other row. Plan for the 6–10% band.
  • Time-value-of-money is now a slider. Default 0% = nominal (as validated). Discounting to today's dollars shrinks far-off costs and resale alike; at ~3–6% it moves the 60-month gap only a few hundred dollars and slightly favors keeping the Kia. Monthly costs are held flat in nominal terms before discounting (gas is your hold-average), so this is an opportunity-cost lens, not a gas-inflation forecast.
  • Resale & energy are the soft inputs. Curves don't auto-haircut for your ~2× mileage (toggle Conservative resale — it's ~symmetric, doesn't change the winner); EV energy is a flat preset (free office charging), so the miles slider scales gas but not EV charging.
  • “Net cost” counts resale equity you can't spend with one car + ~$0 cash. Cross-check the monthly all-in and walk-away-whole columns before deciding.

Bottom line: reliable for the pick — used MY @ 6% cheapest to own, then Ioniq 5 @ 0% / MY @ 0.99%; the lease is pure rent. The size of the edge (used MY @ 6% vs keeping the Kia, 5-yr net) swings with assumptions: ~$11.7k at these defaults · ~$10.5k at gas $4.30 · ~$5.5k under a conservative stack (low gas + winter $120 charging + conservative resale) · and roughly break-even in the worst realistic case (a conventional ~10% used rate plus all of the above) or ~$3.7k if the office charger fails. Translation: the switch wins comfortably if you land a low rate and keep cheap charging; it's a coin flip if you finance at 10% into a pessimistic world. The 6% rate and the charging are the whole bet.

What flips the answer — read before deciding

  • Holding period is the biggest lever. Under ~36 months every buy is still underwater (the rolled $9k Kia gap + WA tax). Over ~48 months the Ioniq 0% wins clean. If you might sell early, the Model Y @ 0.99% is safest — it's the first to go equity-positive (walk-away-whole ~month 33).
  • The lease is rent, not a win. Its low cash-out builds zero equity — you own nothing and can never walk away whole. And at 23,400 mi/yr the mileage overage already inflates it to ~$790–890/mo all-in. Avoid.
  • EV energy assumes your office L2 holds. Cheap ($50) is your normal pattern (free weekday L2 + cheap destination L2, no routine DC). If the office charger is capped/lost, switch the toggle to Office fails $184 — the gap to the Kia shrinks and the PHEV/Kia become the hedge (neither needs a charger).
  • The PHEV is an estimate, and assumes a reachable 120V outlet at your stall. No outlet → it runs as a 38 mpg hybrid and the energy line jumps. Verify the outlet before treating it as the safe option.
  • Ioniq 0% beats taking $7k cash only because the fallback rate is ~6.5%. If you can get sub-5% financing (credit union), take the $7k cash off a lower principal instead.
  • Resale is the soft number. A Tesla price cut or the 70k-mile/3yr haircut (you drive 2× average) trims the Model Y's hold-value edge — toggle Conservative resale to see it.
