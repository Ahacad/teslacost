# Cargo space, in full — 2026 Model Y Premium AWD vs 2025 Sportage X-Line

**July 5, 2026.** The four cargo lines in
[`model-y-vs-sportage-upgrade-2026-07.md`](model-y-vs-sportage-upgrade-2026-07.md)
compressed a real story. This is the full accounting, every number read
straight off the manufacturer's own spec pages (tesla.com owner's manual
Dimensions page + kiamedia.com specifications, both pulled 2026-07-05), plus
the two best instrumented luggage tests. Figures cross-checked against Car and
Driver and What Car?.

## TL;DR

**The Kia wins the number you use weekly; the Tesla wins the max-out number, but
only on its own scorecard.** Behind the raised back seat the Sportage holds
**39.6 vs 29.0 cu ft** — a ~10 cu ft lead, and it's a tall, square, flat hold
that's simply easier to load. The headline "Model Y beats it on max cargo"
(75.5 vs 74.1) is **not a like-for-like comparison**: Tesla's 75.5 counts the
frunk and is defined with only two people aboard. Standard-to-standard (behind
the first row, seats folded) the **Kia still leads 74.1 vs 71.4**; the Model Y
only pulls ahead once you add its 4.1 cu ft frunk. Net: the Sportage is the
better everyday hauler; the Model Y answers back with a sealed drainable frunk,
a deep sub-trunk well, and power-folding seats. Both are genuinely spacious for
the class — this is a real but modest downgrade, not a cliff.

## The official numbers, side by side

Tesla publishes in **liters first** (SAE-adjacent but its own method); Kia
publishes **SAE cu ft**. Both shown; the comparison caveats are below the table.

| Measurement | 2025 Sportage X-Line (gas) | 2026 Model Y Premium AWD (5-seat) |
|---|---|---|
| Behind 2nd row, seats up | **39.6 cu ft** (low floor) / 36.6 (high floor) | 29.0 cu ft (822 L) |
| Behind 1st row, 2nd row folded | **74.1 cu ft** (low) / 69.3 (high) | 71.4 cu ft (2022 L) |
| Front trunk (frunk) | — (none; gas) | **4.1 cu ft** (116 L) |
| Tesla "max total, driver + front pax" | n/a (Kia doesn't quote this) | 75.5 cu ft (2138 L) |
| Tesla "max total, 5 passengers" | n/a | 33.1 cu ft (938 L) |
| Rear-seat split | 60 : 40, fold-flat + recline | 60 : 40, center pass-through, power fold/unfold |
| Underfloor storage | dual-level cargo floor + under-floor well | deep sub-trunk well (counted in 29.0) |
| Power liftgate | **standard on X-Line** (Smart, height-adjustable) | standard (hands-free) |

Sources: Sportage figures identical across every gas trim (LX → X-Pro
Prestige), FWD and AWD alike, per kiamedia specifications. Model Y figures are
the "Premium (5-Seater) and Performance" block of Tesla's owner's manual; the
base Model Y RWD block reads 4.0 / 70.8 / 29.5 / 74.8.

## Read the measurement standards before you compare

This is where the naive "75.5 > 74.1, Tesla wins" falls apart:

1. **75.5 vs 74.1 is apples-to-oranges.** Tesla's *maximum total cargo volume*
   (75.5) **includes the 4.1 cu ft frunk** and is quoted "with driver and front
   passenger" — i.e. only two aboard, whole rear usable. Kia's 74.1 is the plain
   SAE volume behind the first row. Strip the frunk and compare the same box:
   **71.4 (MY) vs 74.1 (Kia) — the Kia leads by 2.7 cu ft.** The Model Y only
   wins the max once the frunk is added back (75.5 vs 74.1, a 1.4 cu ft edge).
2. **The weekly number favors the Kia by ~10.** Behind the raised back seat:
   39.6 vs 29.0. Even with the Kia's dual-level floor raised to its *flat* high
   position (36.6) it still beats the Tesla by 7.6.
3. **The 29.0 has a competing figure.** Tesla's own manual and Car and Driver
   say **29.0**; some outlets (Autoblog's review, a few spec aggregators) cite
   **30.2** from an older/alternate SAE measurement. Worst case for the gap is
   ~9.4 cu ft; best case ~10.6. Either way it's the ~10 you feel.
4. **Kia's numbers assume the floor is low.** The 39.6 / 74.1 pair requires the
   dual-level floor in its low position. Drop it to the high (flat-load) setting
   and you trade ~3 cu ft (39.6 → 36.6) for a flush floor when the seats fold.
5. **Both have real underfloor storage.** Kia: a covered well beneath the
   dual-level floor (spare/tools + shallow stash). Tesla: a **deep sub-trunk
   well** beneath the main boot floor — already inside the 29.0, and the reason
   the Model Y swallows more cases than its on-paper number suggests.

## Frunk and sealed storage — the Tesla's answer

The gas Sportage has no frunk; the engine bay is full. The Model Y's **4.1 cu ft
front trunk** is sealed and **drainable** (Tesla intends it as a cooler/ice
chest), and it's the natural home for charging cables so they never touch the
main cargo floor. Add the sub-trunk well and the Model Y effectively has **three
separate lockable/hidden compartments** (frunk, sub-trunk, main boot) vs the
Sportage's two (under-floor well + main boot). For dividing wet/dirty/valuable
loads this is a genuine Tesla advantage that the volume numbers don't show.

## Seat folding and loading ergonomics

- **Sportage:** 60/40 split that folds flat *and* reclines; manual pulls. Tall,
  square opening with a low-ish load lip and, on the X-Line, a **standard
  height-adjustable Smart power liftgate** (that feature is optional on LX–SX
  Prestige — the X-Line gets it free).
- **Model Y:** 60/40 split where the left control drops the left + center
  sections together and the **center backrest folds independently** (ski/lumber
  pass-through). Seats **power-fold and power-*un*fold** from a switch on the
  left trunk wall, the 16" front screen, or the 8" rear screen — no reaching or
  manual re-latching. Hands-free liftgate standard. The trade is the sloped
  hatchback roofline, which eats vertical space right behind the seats (the
  30.2→29.0 story) and a higher load-over height than the boxy Kia.

## Real-world luggage tests

Different outlets, different bag standards — reported honestly, **not** as a
head-to-head count:

- **Sportage — Autoblog standardized test** (on the Hybrid, 39.5 cu ft ≈ the
  gas car's 39.6): the full six-bag set — two mid-size checked rollers
  (26×16×11 in), two overhead carry-ons (24×15×10), one small roller
  (23×15×10), plus an overnight bag (21×12×12) — **all fit "no problem
  whatsoever,"** and the tester loaded them on the *high* (smaller, 34.5 cu ft)
  floor by mistake, so the low floor has even more room. He calls the hold
  "abundant length and width," class-competitive (the CR-V still looks a touch
  bigger; the Tucson ties it).
- **Model Y — What Car? (Juniper) / Torque News:** roughly **nine carry-on-size
  cases below the parcel shelf, ten with the frunk.** These are carry-on-only
  cases (a smaller, more uniform bag than Autoblog's mixed checked+carry-on
  set), so the counts don't map 1:1 onto the Kia's — but they confirm the deep
  well makes the Model Y punch above its 29.0.

The takeaway both tests agree on: the Kia is the easier **big-square-item**
hauler behind the seats (flat floor, tall walls, more raw volume), while the
Model Y hides surprising capacity in its well and adds a frunk for the overflow.

## Cargo dimensions — what nobody publishes

Neither Kia nor Tesla publishes **linear** cargo dimensions (floor length, width
between the wheel wells, opening height/width), and the reputable instrumented
outlets measure by fitment rather than tape, so there is **no trustworthy
apples-to-apples length/width table** to give. What's documented:

- **Model Y (Tesla manual):** liftgate opens up to ~8 ft (2.4 m) high; overall
  L/W/H 188.6 / 83.8 (mirrors) / 63.9 in; ground clearance 6.6 in. Interior:
  2nd-row leg 40.5, head 38.7, hip 50.6 in.
- **Sportage:** overall L/W 183.5 / 73.4 in (no mirrors); ground clearance 8.3
  in (X-Line); Autoblog's qualitative read is "notably long" cargo floor.
  Interior 2nd-row leg 41.3 in.

If exact box dimensions matter for a specific item (a bike, a dresser), measure
the item against each car in person — the published volumes won't settle it.

## What it means for the switch

- The **~10 cu ft behind-the-seats deficit is the one real, daily practicality
  loss** in going Sportage → Model Y, and the earlier upgrade doc rates it
  correctly as a *mild downgrade*. This deep-dive tightens the claim: the
  "Model Y has more max cargo" line only holds with the frunk counted and on
  Tesla's own two-passenger framing; box-for-box the Kia's folded hold is
  actually larger (74.1 vs 71.4).
- Offsetting it: the frunk (sealed, drainable), the sub-trunk well, three-way
  load separation, and power fold/unfold. For road-trip packing and
  wet/dirty/valuable splitting the Model Y is nicer to *live with*; for a weekly
  Costco run behind an upright back seat the Sportage simply holds more.
- Nothing here changes the cost model — cargo isn't priced — but it sharpens the
  non-money side of the [upgrade
  verdict](model-y-vs-sportage-upgrade-2026-07.md): you are trading ~10 cu ft of
  easy everyday space for two extra sealed compartments and power seats.
