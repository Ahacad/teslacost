Upgrade verdict · Seattle · July 5, 2026

Is the Model Y an upgrade?
Yes, as a machine. No, as a money move.

Setting price aside, the refreshed 2026 Tesla Model Y Premium AWD is a genuinely better car than the 2025 Kia Sportage X-Line it would replace — decisive on speed, tech, safety, quiet, and running cost. Priced honestly it's a ~$320/mo luxury choice (+$19.4k over keeping the Kia across five years), not a saving. And that "clear upgrade" verdict belongs to the new refreshed car — the cheapest way in (a used Model Y) is pre-refresh and loses much of what makes it one.

Is it worth it?

The upgrade is real (Net +30.9). But it costs ~$322/mo. Is that a fair price?

The money is already settled: ~$322/mo is the net cost — it has already credited your fuel savings and subtracted the higher insurance, depreciation, and FSD rent. So what that $322/mo actually buys is the experience bundle: quicker, quieter, smoother, better tech, safer, hands-free on the commute — minus less cargo and no CarPlay. The three real downgrades

Every week

~10 cu ft less cargo

Behind the raised back seat the Sportage holds 39.6 vs 29.0 cu ft — a tall, square, flat hold that's simply easier to load for the Costco run. The MY answers with a frunk and power-fold seats, but loses the weekly volume.

Every drive

No CarPlay / Android Auto

Still unshipped as of June 2026. The most-cited switcher regression — the Kia has it wireless on every trim. Tesla's long-term reviewer: "I got used to it, but I never truly enjoyed it."

Over the years

Depreciation + service

−58% vs −40% over three years, plus Seattle Tesla service means week-plus waits and an active suspension-creak campaign on this exact trim. The Kia is the cheaper, lower-friction thing to own.

Conclusion

Is the Tesla an upgrade? Yes — but know what kind.

As a machine, it's not close. Nine dimensions to the Tesla including the two widest gaps on the board (acceleration and energy cost), against six for the Kia that cluster in cargo and cost-of-ownership. The 2026 Juniper refresh is what earns the verdict: it fixed the old Model Y's harsh ride and loud cabin, the two things that used to make this an even fight.

As a purchase, it's a want, not a saving. The build costs ~$322/mo more than keeping the Kia — roughly the price of a luxury upgrade you'd pay happily if you value the speed, quiet, and tech, and can absorb the ~10 cu ft cargo cut and the missing CarPlay. If the goal is money, keep the Kia; if it's the driving experience, the Tesla delivers it and then some.

The catch on the cheap path

The one way to make this pencil out — a used Model Y (−$4.4k vs the Kia) — buys a pre-refresh car. A 2022–23 build has the harsh ride, the louder 67–71 dB cabin, no rear screen, and it's the exact population the TÜV suspension data indicts. You'd get the powertrain and tech upgrade, but sacrifice most of what makes the new car the clear upgrade above. Discount the used path's "upgrade" accordingly.
