# Is the Model Y an upgrade? — 2026 MY Premium AWD vs 2025 Sportage X-Line

**July 4, 2026.** Question: setting money aside, is the refreshed 2026 Tesla
Model Y Premium AWD actually a better car than the 2025 Kia Sportage X-Line it
would replace? Method: 6 research dimensions, 23 agents, every load-bearing
claim independently re-verified (2 of 15 checks refuted or materially corrected
the finding — both corrections are in here).

## TL;DR

**Yes — a real upgrade as a machine, and specifically because of the 2026
"Juniper" refresh.** Decisive wins on performance, tech, crash safety, cabin
noise, and running cost. Three concrete downgrades: ~10 cu ft less everyday
cargo, no CarPlay, and depreciation + service friction. Priced by the cost
model it is a **~$320/mo luxury choice** (+$19.4k vs keeping the Kia over 5
years, `my099` vs `kia` @60mo), not a money move. And the "clear upgrade"
verdict only fully applies to the **new refreshed car** — the model's cheapest
path (used MY) is a pre-refresh build that keeps the harsh ride and louder
cabin the refresh fixed.

## Performance & refinement — massive upgrade

- 0–60: 3.9 s measured (Car and Driver instrumented test of the refreshed
  Long Range/Premium AWD, Aug 8 2025; quarter mile 12.3 s @ 115 mph) vs ~10 s
  for the Sportage 2.5L — CR: "one of the few vehicles today that needs 10
  seconds," Edmunds: "downright slow," 8-speed "isn't the smoothest shifter."
  Passing response is the gas car's weakest area and the EV's strongest.
- The refresh removed the old Model Y's two disqualifiers. Ride: the same
  Edmunds editor who called the 2020 MY Performance "the worst-riding car I've
  ever driven in my entire life" rates the new suspension "light-years better";
  KBB/InsideEVs call it comfortable if not plush (still firm-side — the
  Premium AWD rides on 19"/20" wheels). Noise: acoustic glass all around;
  independent meters read ~64–66 dB @ 70 mph vs ~67–71 dB pre-refresh.
- Body structure stiffened 3%, suspension retuned — the 2025–26 ride reviews
  diverging from 2020–24 reviews is a hardware change, not press-kit spin.

## Tech & software — clear upgrade, two paid asterisks

- 15.4" front + 8" rear touchscreens, monthly whole-vehicle OTA, phone-as-key,
  Sentry Mode, remote preconditioning vs one 12.3" screen with
  infotainment-only OTA. The US refresh restored a turn-signal stalk (signals
  only; gear selection on screen).
- **Verifier refuted the "standard Autopilot" framing** — as of Jan 23 2026
  Tesla ships new US Model Ys with only Traffic-Aware Cruise Control free;
  lane-centering itself requires the $99/mo FSD (Supervised) subscription
  (one-time purchase ended Feb 14 2026; HW4 runs v14.3.3, supervised city
  streets + lane changes). The X-Line has standard hands-on Lane Following
  Assist on any marked road (HDA/adaptive cruise start at SX trim). Net:
  **with the FSD sub the ADAS gap is a generation; without it the Tesla's
  standard kit is arguably weaker than the Kia's.** The build carries the sub
  (`S.fsd`), so the model already prices this at $109/mo.
- **Still no CarPlay/Android Auto as of June 2026** — the leaked windowed
  CarPlay remains unshipped (now waiting on iOS 26 adoption, per spring-2026
  reporting). Edmunds' long-term 2026 MY reviewer: "I got used to it, but I
  never truly enjoyed it" (music-queue and voice-to-text are the gripes).
  Most-cited switcher regression; the Sportage has it wireless on every trim.

## Crash safety — mild upgrade

- Refreshed MY: NHTSA 5-star overall and in every sub-test (verified against
  the live NHTSA API, all four MY2026 variants; AWD rollover risk 8.6%),
  announced Feb 10 2026. Euro NCAP 5-star Nov 19 2025 (Adult 91 / Child 93 /
  VRU 86 / Assist 92 — tested variant was the Premium AWD) and Best in Class
  2025 Small SUV. Correction from verification: it was *not* 2025's highest
  overall score — the Mercedes CLA edged it in the December round; that
  superlative came from Tesla's blog.
- 2025 Sportage: plain IIHS Top Safety Pick, not TSP+. Verification corrected
  the mechanism: the blocker is the updated moderate-overlap rear-passenger
  test (no qualifying rating), fixed only in post-May-2025 2026 builds.
- Note: the refreshed MY is not on the IIHS 2026 TSP list — its 2025 TSP+
  rests on pre-refresh builds. NHTSA/Euro NCAP are the refresh-specific data.

## Reliability & service — wash

- Consumer Reports (Dec 2025): Tesla 9th of all brands — one spot **ahead** of
  Kia (10th) — and the Model Y is "the most reliable EV" (81/100 predicted).
  Caveat from verification: that score is a *prediction* built on 2023–25
  pre-refresh owner data; the refresh's new suspension/controls aren't
  directly measured yet.
- Counterweights: JD Power 2026 VDS (3-yr-old cars) favors Kia 193 vs 226
  PP100. Germany's TUV Report 2026 ranked the (pre-refresh) Model Y **dead
  last** among 2–3-yr-old cars — 17.3% failing inspection, chiefly axle
  suspension and brakes. The refresh year logged 6 NHTSA recalls (none
  OTA-fixable) vs one Sportage recall that doesn't touch a gas X-Line.
- The exact trim has an **active suspension-creak service campaign**
  (SB-26-31-003 / SB-25-31-005, May 8 2026: front upper control-arm mounts +
  both dampers, ~4 h labor, warranty). Seattle service: week-plus waits for
  non-emergency work are common (SoDo/Renton/Lynnwood/Bellevue), partly offset
  by mobile service. KBB owners: 80% of 89 reviews recommend; quality rated
  the weakest attribute.

## Space & practicality — mild downgrade

- Behind row 2 the Sportage wins by ~10 cu ft: 39.6 vs ~29.0 cu ft (both
  verified against official spec sheets; worst case for the gap is 9.4 cu ft
  if the 30.2 source family is right). That's the number you feel weekly.
- Seats folded the MY pulls slightly ahead once the frunk counts: 75.5 vs
  74.1 cu ft — plus a drainable frunk (cooler), deep sub-trunk well, and
  power-fold/reclining rear seats vs the Kia's manual bench.
- Rear-seat room is a near-wash (legroom 41.3 vs 40.5 in; headroom tied).
  Roof load is a wash (165 lb both). Ground clearance is not: 8.3 vs 6.6 in —
  it matters for forest-service roads and deep-snow days.

## Towing & road trips — mixed, leaning upgrade

- Ratings: MY 3,500 lb / 350 lb tongue with the Tow Package (in the build) vs
  Sportage 2,500 lb braked / 1,653 lb unbraked — 40% more, verified against
  the owner's manual and Kia media specs. Aftermarket hitches do NOT unlock
  Tesla's Trailer Mode or rating.
- Towing range: expect ~50% loss with a boxy trailer. Verification corrected
  the viral number: the InsideEVs U-Haul test (Nov 2023, pre-refresh) towed
  ~1,250 lb — not 2,250 — at exactly doubled consumption, ~100 mi legs at
  65 mph. The 327-mi Juniper should floor around 120–150 mi/charge boxy,
  ~200+ mi with an aero camper. Frequent long-distance towing flips this
  dimension to a downgrade.
- Trailer-free PNW trips are a near-wash: 84 Supercharger stations / 946
  stalls in WA incl. Snoqualmie Pass; Seattle–Portland nonstop,
  Seattle–Spokane one 15–25 min stop. Heat-pump winter loss ~14% typical,
  25–30% worst-case — mild Seattle winters sit at the low end.
- Running cost is a landslide: at $5.362/gal (AAA Seattle, 7/4/26) the
  Sportage burns ~$5,020/yr at 23,400 mi; off-peak home charging (Seattle
  City Light TOU 8.05¢/kWh, midnight–6am) runs ~$530/yr. **The model's gas
  default is $4.60 — drag the gas slider to ~$5.36 to price today's reality**
  (it understates the Kia's fuel bill and thus flatters keeping it).

## Depreciation — the clear downgrade (already priced in the model)

- CarEdge: MY −58.1% over 3 years vs Sportage −40.0% — an 18-point retention
  gap, verified live and cross-checked; iSeeCars' dataset implies ~30 points.
  Treat 18 as the conservative end of a real 15–30-point disadvantage.
- Caveat: those are projections off 2020–25 history distorted by Tesla's own
  MSRP cuts and the Juniper launch; used-MY prices stabilized in late
  2025–early 2026, and friendlier forecasts put a Juniper at 45–50% retained.
  The model's `my099` resale curve already embeds this class of decline.

## What adversarial verification changed

Per the verify-the-verifiers rule, the material corrections, kept visible:

1. **REFUTED — "every Model Y includes Autosteer free."** False since
   Jan 23 2026; TACC only. Lane-centering is inside the $99/mo FSD sub. Also
   corrected the Kia side: X-Line has LFA, not HDA. (Flips the no-FSD ADAS
   comparison toward the Kia.)
2. **REFUTED — forum quote "the Sportage rides substantially more smoothly"**
   attributed to a TMC cross-shop thread. Fetched all 3 pages: the quote does
   not exist; the thread actually ranks the refreshed MY's ride second only to
   the CR-V Hybrid and quietest of the group. Any remaining Sportage comfort
   edge is contested, not established.
3. Corrected superlatives/numbers: Euro NCAP "highest score of 2025" (Mercedes
   CLA took it in December); Sportage folded cargo is top-3, not class-leading
   (CR-V 76.5); the U-Haul towing test was ~1,250 lb not 2,250; CR's 81/100 is
   a prediction resting on pre-refresh data; TSP+ blocker was rear-passenger
   protection, not headlights.

## Tie to the cost model

- Monthly cash flow is nearly identical: `my099` $1,538/mo all-in vs Kia
  $1,587 — the +$19.4k @ 5 yr is depreciation + insurance + FSD, not the
  payment. The upgrade therefore costs ≈ **$322/mo** (19.4k/60) on net-cost
  terms.
- The used-MY rows (`usedmy6`/`usedmy`) buy the powertrain + tech upgrade at
  the best price, **but a used 2022–23 car is pre-refresh**: harsher ride,
  louder cabin (67–71 dB), no rear screen, and it's the population the TUV
  suspension data indicts. The refresh is a meaningful chunk of what makes
  the new car an upgrade — discount the used path's "upgrade" accordingly.
- Current-reality knobs: gas slider → $5.36; insurance already at the real
  quotes (see `insurance-reality-check-2026-07.md`).
